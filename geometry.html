Motivation. Let $S$ be a smooth surface in $\R^3$ and $\gamma:I\to S$ be a curve on $S$. Then $\disp\len(\gamma)=\int_I \abs{\dot\gamma}$.
To compute this in practice, we have a (not necessarily complete) parametrization $X:D\to S$ with $D\sub \R^2$. We call the coordinate variables in $\R^2$ $u,v$ to avoid confusion.
Taking $\gamma(t) = X(u(t),v(t))$, we have $\dot\gamma(t) = X_u u'(t) + X_v v'(t)$ and so $\disp\abs{\dot\gamma(t)}^2=u'(t)^2\abs{X_u}^2 + 2u'(t)v'(t)X_u\cdot X_v + v'(t)^2\abs{X_v}^2$.
Similarly, to compute the surface area, we have (assuming $X$ is a complete parametrization) $\disp \area(S)=\int_D \abs{X_u\times X_v}\d u\d v=\int_D \sqrt{\abs{X_u}^2\abs{X_v}^2-(X_u\cdot X_v)^2}\d u\d v$

Definition. Given a parametrization $X:D\to S$, the first fundamental form $I$ is the Gram matrix of the partials $X_u,X_v$. Namely $I=\begin{bmatrix} E & F\\ F & G \end{bmatrix}$ where $E=\abs{X_u}^2$, $F=X_u\cdot X_v$, and $G=\abs{X_v}^2$.

In these terms $\sqrt{EG-F^2}=\sqrt{\det I}$ is the area of the parallelogram spanned by $X_u$ and $X_v$, so that $\area=\int\int \sqrt{EG-F^2}$. Similarly, $\len = \int \sqrt{u'^2 E + 2u'v' G + v'^2 F}$.

Exercise. Use the above to compute the surface areas of spheres and tori.

Motivation. For curves in $2$d, the curvature measured the rate of change of the tangent line changed. So for surfaces in $3$d, we'd like to measure how the tangent plane changes. Notice that we have two dimensions in which we can move the point and measure the change of the tangent plane. For example, if $S$ is a cylinder around the $z$ axis, then moving a point up does not change the tangent plane, while moving a point around does.

We can define the tangent plane of $S$ at $p$ as the plane of all derivatives of curves through $p$ on $S$. Computationally, if $S$ is given by $F(x,y,z)=0$ where $\nabla F\neq 0$, then the tangent plane at $p$ is the plane orthogonal to $\nabla F|_p$.

Definition. A smooth surface $S$ is orientable if there exists a smooth map $N:S\to \S^2$ for which $N(p) \perp T_p(S)$ at all $p\in S$. This makes it so $T_p(S)$ and $T_{N(p)}(\S^2)$ are parallel.

Exercise. Show that $S$ is always locally orientable. Moreover, there are only two possibilities for the map $N$, namely $N$ and $-N$.

Temporary definition. The curvature of an orientable smooth surface $S$ at a point $p$ is the differential $\d N: T_pS\to T_{N(p)}\S^2$. Given a parametrization $X$, we can take $N=\dfrac{X_u\times X_v}{\abs{X_u\times X_v}}$, with $N_u = aX_u + cX_v$ and $N_v = bX_u + dX_v$. Then we can think of $\d N$ as the matrix $\begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$, where this of course depends on $u,v$.

Example. The cylinder $x^2+y^2=R^2$. We have $X(u,v)=(R\cos u,R\sin u,v)$. Thus $X_u = (-R\sin u,R\cos u,0)$ and $X_v = (0,0,1)$ and $N=(\cos u,\sin u,0)$. Therefore $N_v=0$, and $N_u=X_u/R$. Thus $\d N$ is given by $\diag(1/R,0)$, as we might expect.

Exercise. The curvature of $z=y^2-x^2$ at the origin is $\diag(2,-2)$.

Claim. $\d N$ defines a symmetric bilinear form on $T_p(S)$, in the sense that $N_u\cdot X_v=N_v\cdot X_u$. In particular, it has two real eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$, and these are the principal curvatures. Their product, the determinant of $\d N$ is the Gaussian curvature denoted $K=\lambda_1\lambda_2$. This is independent of the sign choice of $N$ or the parametrization.

Exercise. Let $X:D\to S$ be a parametrization where the orientation $N$ is injective on $X(D)$ and the Gaussian curvature does not vanish. Then the area covered on $\S^2$ as $N(X(D))$ equals $\int_D \abs{K} \abs{X_u\times X_v}$.
In particular, $\abs{K_p}$ is the limit of $\area(N(A))/\area(A)$ as $A$ shrinks to $\{p\}$.
